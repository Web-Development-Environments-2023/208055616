Q&A

Q1

Three main differences between Web 1.0, Web 2.0, and Web 3.0 are:

Web 1.0, also known as the static web, was a one-way communication where users could only consume content, whereas Web 2.0 introduced user-generated content, social media, and two-way communication.

Web 2.0 also brought about the rise of mobile computing and the use of mobile devices to access the internet, which was not as prevalent during the Web 1.0 era.

Web 3.0, also known as the semantic web, is a future vision of the internet that aims to make the web more intelligent and able to understand and interpret human language. It will enable machines to understand the meaning of web content and provide more personalized, relevant results.

Q2

The differences in query results for "side effects of medications" in Web 1.0 and Web 3.0 may include:

Web 1.0 search results may provide basic information about side effects, such as a list of common side effects or a definition of what side effects are. The information may be presented in a static, text-based format with limited interactivity.

Web 3.0 search results may use semantic technologies to understand the context of the query and provide more personalized, relevant results based on the user's location, medical history, and other factors. The results may include interactive elements such as videos, chatbots, or voice assistants to help users better understand and manage medication side effects.

Web 3.0 search results may also leverage blockchain technology to provide a more secure and transparent way of sharing medical data and information about medication side effects between patients, healthcare providers, and pharmaceutical companies.
